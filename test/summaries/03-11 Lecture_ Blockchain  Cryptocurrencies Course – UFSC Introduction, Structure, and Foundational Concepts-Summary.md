4. Foundational Technology: Nothing Here Is New
All the building blocks of blockchain predate Bitcoin by decades; the true
innovation of 2008¿2009 was the game-theoretic protocol design that stitched
those components together into a self-reinforcing system where cheating is
economically irrational.
Data structures (linked lists, pointers): 1950s.
Hashes as indexes and integrity controls: 1960s.
Public-key cryptography: publicly known from 1977 (RSA); later revealed that
British intelligence used it as early as 1972.
Peer-to-peer networking: 1994.
Commitments (hash-and-reveal schemes): 1980s.
Homomorphic encryption: ~1997¿1998; zero-knowledge proofs: ~2004.
The genuine novelty of Bitcoin/blockchain is the game-theoretic glue: the
protocol design ensures that following the rules is the dominant strategy and
cheating results in total loss.
Technically, a blockchain is a linked list where pointers are replaced by hash
pointers, providing integrity control over each successive element.
5. Three Pillars of Cryptocurrencies
Blockchain-based systems are stable when three mutually reinforcing pillars ¿
History/Integrity, Rules, and Value/Incentives ¿ are all functioning; weakness
in any one pillar destabilizes the whole ecosystem.
History/Integrity: The chain¿s immutability ensures that recorded events
cannot be altered retroactively.
Rules: Protocol rules (consensus mechanisms, smart contract logic) govern
what can and cannot happen on the chain.
Value/Incentives: Participants must have an economic or systemic reason to
follow the rules; incentive engineering is especially challenging for nonfinancial applications.
For non-financial applications, designing clear incentives is significantly harder
than for currency-based systems and is a core topic of the course.
6. Cryptographic Primitives & Key Concepts
Students will build up from classical primitives (hashes, public-key
cryptography) through modern constructs (zero-knowledge proofs,
homomorphic encryption, commitments) to understand why blockchain
properties such as immutability, privacy, and trustless execution actually hold.
A wallet = a public key; understanding public-key cryptography is essential to
demystify basic cryptocurrency terminology.
Hash pointers replace standard pointers in the blockchain data structure,
enabling integrity verification.
Commitments: publish a hash now; reveal the pre-image later ¿ secondpreimage resistance guarantees the commitment is binding.
Zero-knowledge proofs (ZKPs): allow one party to prove knowledge of
information without revealing the information itself; foundational to modern
privacy-preserving blockchain protocols.
Homomorphic encryption: enables computation on encrypted data ¿
increasingly relevant to blockchain applications.
David Chaum invented blind signatures: signing a document inside an
envelope ¿ you sign something you never see ¿ enabling anonymous digital
money and electronic elections.
The course will also cover consensus mechanisms: Proof of Work, Proof of
Stake, and Byzantine Agreement protocols (over 50 catalogued types exist).
Cryptographic identification of users does not require de-anonymization ¿
privacy and identity can coexist.

11. Drex (Brazil¿s CBDC) ¿ Rise, Fall, and Implications
Brazil¿s Central Bank Digital Currency, Drex, was officially discontinued on
August 5th of the prior year at Blockchain in Rio, announced by Gabriel
Galipolu. The reasons were structural: public blockchains by design lack
transaction privacy, prevent the central bank from controlling wallet balances,
and resist regulation ¿ properties that are features for cryptocurrency
advocates but fatal flaws for a central bank instrument. This opened the
Brazilian stablecoin market.
Core incompatibilities: no transaction privacy, no balance privacy, and
insufficient regulatory control ¿ all inherent to public blockchain design.
Bitcoin¿s genesis block embeds the front page of a 2008 newspaper criticizing
central banks, making the philosophical opposition to central banking a
permanent, irremovable part of the protocol.
¿Drex¿ unintentionally means ¿shit¿ (plural) in Hebrew (drek/drex), which caused
public backlash from the Israeli community when the name was announced.
With Drex gone, Brazilian stablecoin developers can now compete in the
market without facing government-backed competition; at least nine Brazilian
Real-denominated stablecoins currently exist, including one by the instructor.

14. Grading & Evaluation Structure
The semester grade is calculated from a combination of one theoretical exam
and a series of practical partial deliveries. This distributed approach was
deliberately chosen over a single final submission to prevent last-minute work
and ensure ongoing engagement and quality.
The university requires at least one exam and a final grade for each student; a
single exam is considered unfair by the instructor.
Practical partial deliveries replace a single end-of-semester project submission;
they allow for tweaking and course corrections if things go off track.
Deliverable deadlines are generally set two weeks after the relevant lecture, at
23:59 on a Wednesday, and will be posted on Moodle.
The theoretical test is on July 1, conducted in a lab via Moodle Provas:
approximately 12 questions, one hour, covering everything discussed
throughout the semester.
Final projects must be submitted by July 6 so the instructor can mark them by
July 7.
The SINOVA competition finals are on July 10; two selected teams represent the
course.
Attending an external competition earns 0.5 bonus points; winning earns 1.0
bonus point.
Recovery sessions are scheduled for July 13; final grades will be issued around
July 8.
