Blockchain Opportunities: From
Programmable Money to Global Careers
This event, held at the Federal University of Santa Catarina (UFSC) and
organized by Blockchain Rio, delivered a comprehensive exploration of Brazil¿s
evolving financial infrastructure, academic blockchain research, and global
career opportunities in the Web3 ecosystem. Jean Martina argued that while
PIX is a landmark achievement, a programmable Real stablecoin is the
essential next step for Brazil¿s financial sovereignty. Lucas Palma showcased
LabSec¿s decade-long blockchain research portfolio spanning non-financial
applications. A diverse industry panel then shared personal journeys and
market insights, collectively affirming that the blockchain revolution is
irreversible and that the demand for talent across all disciplines far exceeds
current supply.
The Case for a Programmable Real
Jean Martina opened with a deliberately provocative question: does Brazil actually
need a stablecoin, given that PIX already exists? The framing set the stage for a
nuanced argument that these two technologies are not rivals but occupy
fundamentally different layers of a financial stack.
PIX: A Cultural Revolution, Not Just a Technology
Jean began by establishing the undeniable scale of PIX¿s success. By the numbers:
over 170 million people have made at least one PIX transaction, more than 20
million companies operate with it, and the system moves 35 trillion Brazilian Reais
per year. Technologically, he described PIX as conceptually similar to a traditional
TED transfer ¿ just executed in real time, with analogous message structures. Its
true power, however, is socio-technical: PIX fundamentally changed Brazilian culture
by making digital money the default. The combination of an extremely low-friction
interface and zero cost for individuals (though not zero cost for the Central Bank
or for businesses, which do face operational costs, albeit far lower than credit card
acquiring fees) drove mass adoption to the point where millions of Brazilians no
longer carry physical cash.
Jean contextualized PIX within a broader arc of Brazilian financial revolutions: the
emergence of credit cooperatives in the 1990s, the rise of fintechs like Nubank
and Inter around 2010 (which built bank-like products outside traditional banking
regulation), and now a third financial revolution centered on on-chain
infrastructure ¿ one that moves beyond both banks and fintechs entirely.
Where PIX Falls Short: The Programmability Gap
Despite its success, PIX has a critical limitation: it moves balances, not logic. Its
scope is explicitly defined as moving funds between regulated financial accounts
within Brazil, primarily for daily payments. It operates on a domestic liquidation
model, requiring institutions to register with the Central Bank. Furthermore, while
PIX technically functions 24/7/365, Jean clarified that the actual registration of
transactions only occurs on banking days ¿ a meaningful distinction from truly
continuous settlement. He also raised a long-term sustainability concern: the
Central Bank president acknowledged at Blockchain Rio the previous August that
PIX costs the Central Bank a fortune to maintain, and there are credible concerns
that public budgets may eventually force a reduction in transaction speed
tolerances.
Jean also made a point often overlooked: most countries in the world ¿ including
Costa Rica, Colombia, and Argentina ¿ already have payment rails equivalent to
PIX. Instant payment infrastructure is becoming a global standard, not a
competitive differentiator.
Stablecoins: Money That Thinks
The core distinction Jean drew is that a stablecoin ¿ a cryptocurrency maintaining
a one-to-one parity with a fiat currency ¿ can do something PIX cannot: it can carry
embedded logic. This is the concept of programmable money. When a payment
arrives in a stablecoin, the money itself can be pre-programmed to automatically
split into investments: for example, allocating a portion to a CDB, another portion to
crypto, and the remainder to savings ¿ all executed autonomously via smart
contracts, potentially governed by AI agents.
Stablecoins also operate 24/7/365 in a global, permissionless environment,
enabling their use as digital assets anywhere in the world, not just within Brazil¿s
regulatory perimeter. This opens the door to market operations, investments,
tokenization of receivables and physical assets, and entirely new financial
paradigms.
Global Stablecoin Landscape and the Brazilian Opportunity
Jean surveyed four distinct global use cases that illustrate stablecoins¿ real-world
utility:
Argentina: The world¿s leading stablecoin adopter, driven by severe exchange
restrictions and chronic inflation ¿ stablecoins serve as a shield against
currency devaluation.
Nigeria: Uses stablecoins to circumvent government exchange controls and
maintain a functioning parallel exchange rate.
Turkey: Heavily relies on stablecoins for remittances, allowing workers across
Europe to send money to families back home efficiently.
Singapore: Employs stablecoins in an institutionalized model to fractionalize
public bonds and government-controlled titles.
The global stablecoin market is experiencing explosive growth, with 80% of all
emissions denominated in US dollars (dominated by USDT from Tether and USDC
from Circle). However, a crucial and often overlooked trend is that local-currency
stablecoin growth is running three times faster than dollar stablecoin growth ¿ a
clear signal of maturing market demand for non-dollar programmable money.
For Brazil, a watershed moment came when the Central Bank president announced
at the previous year¿s Blockchain Rio event that the Drex digital Real project would
be discontinued as a currency, having faced architectural limitations. This decision
created a significant market vacuum, which a wave of private stablecoin initiatives is
now rushing to fill. Jean noted that Brazil¿s regulatory environment is actively
supportive of this transition, with the Central Bank designing a tiered licensing
framework that will accommodate players of varying sizes. Major Brazilian banks
and financial institutions are already placing bets on stablecoin initiatives,
recognizing them as the next foundational financial rail.
The Value Proposition of Local Stablecoins
Jean argued forcefully that digital and economic sovereignty demand a local
stablecoin. No scalable national financial system can be built on foreign currency ¿
dollarization may solve short-term problems but ultimately places a nation¿s
monetary policy under foreign tutelage. More practically: Brazilians live, earn, and
spend in Reais. A vendor at a street fair does not accept dollars. If a holder of dollarpegged stablecoins faces a 5% currency movement on a given day, they can lose an
entire year¿s worth of investment yield in hours.
The key use cases Jean identified for a Real stablecoin in Brazil include:
Savings and Yield: Generating returns from idle money that can exceed
traditional instruments, programmatically and automatically.
Efficient FX: Executing currency exchange directly via smart contracts, without
intermediary applications or accounts.
Native Tokenization: Converting any asset with assigned value ¿ real estate,
livestock, receivables ¿ into fractionalized, Real-denominated digital tokens
transferable anywhere.
24/7 Treasury Management: Perpetually optimizing the balance between
invested capital and available liquidity.
On-chain Neo-banks: A new generation of banking applications that look and
feel like Nubank but run entirely on-chain, converting deposits into digital Real
and sourcing balances from the blockchain.
Nora Finance and the Float-Based Business Model
Jean introduced Nora Finance, a stablecoin co-founded by students and professors
(including himself) at the university. Nora Finance¿s core product is the BRS ¿ a
programmable Real designed to operate across multiple blockchain networks with
a mint-and-burn mechanism. Its regulatory compliance layer sits within the
execution structure, with an ambition to evolve toward a 100% DeFi model with onchain liquidity.
The strategic thesis behind Nora Finance directly challenges the prevailing
stablecoin business model. Today, most stablecoins are effectively worth less than
face value once transaction fees are accounted for ¿ a spread of roughly 5 cents in
and 5 cents out means 1 Real effectively becomes 90 centavos. Nora Finance¿s
counter-thesis is elegantly simple: generate revenue from the float¿s treasury
rather than from fees. If 100 million BRS are in circulation and those funds are
invested in Tesouro Direto at Brazil¿s Selic rate of approximately 15% annually, the
treasury earns 15 million Reais per year ¿ sufficient to operate the business
without charging any transaction fees. Going further, a portion of that return can be
distributed as yield to BRS holders, creating a stablecoin that not only transacts at
par (1 BRS = 1 BRL, always) but also pays interest to its holders. Jean¿s conviction is
that a frictionless, zero-fee, interest-paying open rail will naturally win the market
precisely because it is permissionless and non-proprietary.
PIX and Stablecoins: A Stack, Not a Competition
Jean concluded this section by reframing the entire discussion. PIX is not a
competitor to stablecoins. They inhabit different layers of the same financial stack.
PIX serves as the on-ramp and off-ramp between the traditional financial system
and the on-chain world. A grandmother making a PIX payment feeds into a Real
Digital layer, which can then tokenize an asset ¿ making her an indirect participant
in the on-chain economy without ever knowing it. The future is a layered
architecture: PIX handles fiat-to-digital conversion; programmable Real carries
embedded logic; and tokenized assets sit at the apex, enabling innovation not yet
conceived. As Jean put it: ¿PIX makes money circulate better. Stablecoins make
money function as software. The possibilities are infinite.¿
Blockchain Research and Non-Financial Applications
Lucas Palma offered a panoramic view of blockchain¿s academic frontier,
demonstrating that the technology¿s relevance extends well beyond financial
markets. His presentation served as both an institutional record and an invitation to
students seeking research paths in computing and systems.
LabSec: Two Decades of Security Research
The Laboratory of Security and Computing (LabSec) at UFSC has operated for
more than two decades, founded in 2001 within the Department of Informatics and
Statistics. Its core specialties span digital certification, public key infrastructure
(PKI), digital signatures, electronic documents, cryptographic protocols, and
ceremonies ¿ with blockchain and, more recently, verifiable credentials added to
its research portfolio.
As of 2026, the laboratory comprises four associated professors (three from the
Florianópolis campus in computer science, one from the Aranguá campus in
computer engineering), one post-doctoral student, three CLT employees, more
than 10 administrative education technicians (TAIs) who are UFSC civil servants,
over 20 postgraduate students (master¿s and doctoral), and more than 30
undergraduate students actively engaged in project development.
The Blockchain Working Group: From 2017 to Today
Blockchain became a structured, collaborative pursuit within LabSec in 2017, when
Lucas Palma, Professor Jean Martina, and Professor Martim Vigil (Aranguá campus)
formed a dedicated working group. The timing was notable: 2017 marked the first
significant deflation of the blockchain hype cycle following the post-Ethereum
growth surge, a period Lucas characterized as clarifying ¿ separating genuine
technological potential from inflated expectations. Today, blockchain has entered
what Lucas described as a stabilization phase, where its real-world direction is
clearer and the technology is maturing into reliable infrastructure.
The academic output of this working group is substantial:
Over 20 undergraduate TCC theses in blockchain across computer science and
information systems.
Seven master¿s theses in total: five already defended, two currently in progress
(Arthur Kripa and Lucas Costa).
Three doctoral theses: one defended in 2024 (Lucas Palma¿s own work), with
two others in active development by Johan Vesco and Gabriel Estevam.
Representative completed master¿s theses include Luca Camperi¿s ¿Protocolo de
Diploma Digital Autosoberano com Rastreabilidade Tecnológica¿ and a work titled
¿Blockchain Privacy and Scalability in the Decentralized Validity Energy Trading Context
with Hyperledger Fabric¿. All works are publicly accessible through the UFSC
institutional repository and the UFSC TCC website.
Project Spotlight 1: Timestamping on Blockchain (2019¿2020)
The first highlighted project, ¿Tempestividade em Blockchain¿ (Timeliness in
Blockchain), was financed by Brai Tecnologia, a Florianópolis-based company. The
research question was deceptively precise: given that different blockchain platforms
generate blocks at varying intervals (Bitcoin in minutes, Ethereum in seconds), how
accurately can a blockchain serve as a trusted timestamping authority?
The team proposed a model in which a document¿s cryptographic hash is broadcast
to the network, and participating nodes use a form of decentralized voting to
determine the precise moment the document first appeared ¿ averaging out
detection timestamps to cancel individual node errors. The result was a working
prototype delivered to Brai Tecnologia that produced timestamps with accuracy
comparable to classical timestamping infrastructure, even on networks with
relatively large block generation windows. The project demonstrated that
blockchain-based timestamping is not merely theoretically viable but practically
achievable.
Project Spotlight 2: Post-Quantum Cryptography and ZKP for Blockchain
(2019¿2020)
The second project, conducted in partnership with Arison ¿ a company then
developing a new blockchain platform ¿ took a forward-looking stance on
cryptographic resilience. LabSec delivered protocols for converting classical
cryptographic algorithms to post-quantum alternatives, designed to protect
blockchain systems against attacks that quantum computers will eventually be
capable of executing. At the time, quantum computing was a more distant concern;
today, it is an increasingly proximate reality. The project simultaneously
investigated the application of zero-knowledge proofs (ZKP) within blockchain
contexts ¿ a technology that has since moved from academic curiosity to
production-grade deployment across multiple Layer 2 networks. Lucas emphasized
that both post-quantum resilience and ZKP will become progressively more critical
as the blockchain ecosystem matures.
Project Spotlight 3: Higher Education Data Traceability Network (2022¿
Present)
The most extensive and ongoing project is the ¿Rede Rastreabilidade Dados da
Educação Superior¿ (Higher Education Data Traceability Network), financed by
Brazil¿s Ministry of Education and running since late 2022. The project addresses a
structural gap in Brazil¿s higher education governance: the Ministry currently bases
most of its decisions on bi-annual census data, meaning it perpetually operates
with information that is years old.
The proposed solution is a distributed, decentralized data bus through which
higher education institutions can share data with each other and with the Ministry
in real time. While the presence of a central regulator (the Ministry) might seem to
contradict decentralization principles, Lucas explained that Brazil¿s higher education
system is in fact partially decentralized by design ¿ institutions retain significant
administrative autonomy, including over data custody and sharing. The blockchain
layer enables sharing without ceding that autonomy.
The network is built on Hyperledger Fabric, a framework from the Linux
Foundation Decentralized Trust ecosystem. Hyperledger Fabric was chosen
specifically for its ability to create segmented data channels ¿ supporting both a
public view accessible to all participants and private sub-networks isolating
sensitive data ¿ a critical requirement for compliance with Brazil¿s Lei Geral de
Proteção de Dados (LGPD). A key practical benefit of the system is its capacity to
detect and reduce fraud in the issuance of academic documents, including
diplomas and academic transcripts.
Lucas described the project as a daily exercise in navigating genuinely conflicting
requirements: leveraging blockchain¿s transparency and immutability while
simultaneously enforcing the data privacy mandates that modern regulation
demands. This tension, he suggested, is precisely what makes the research
intellectually valuable and professionally relevant.
Panel Discussion: Opportunities in the Web3
Ecosystem
Moderated by Francisco Carvalho (Blockchain Rio), the panel brought together five
professionals whose paths into the blockchain industry were as diverse as the roles
they now occupy ¿ a powerful testament to the breadth of opportunity the
ecosystem offers.
Panelist Introductions
Solange Parisotto is an economist with over 15 years in financial services, currently
working at Sicredi, a credit cooperative. Cooperatives, she noted, differ structurally
from traditional banks ¿ members are co-owners, creating a distinct trust dynamic
that enables innovation. Solange participated in Phase 2 of the Drex pilot and will
be involved in the forthcoming Phase 3, which will focus heavily on credit. Her
expertise lies in translating technology capabilities into real-world business value.
Hamilton Haskell, co-founder and CMO of Chainless, encountered Bitcoin in late
2013 ¿ among the earliest adopters in his social circle. He entered university in
2015 to study physics, where he was famously ¿the only person talking about
Bitcoin.¿ That single listener became his business partner, and together they have
been building in crypto since 2018, through two failed ventures to their current one.
Chainless is an on-chain neo-bank that uses PIX as the on-ramp, converts deposits
into stablecoins, and leverages account abstraction (a mechanism championed by
Ethereum¿s Vitalik Buterin) to make DeFi interactions dramatically simpler. As of the
event, Chainless had reached 31,000 wallets created and processed over $10
million in volume in February alone.
Bianca Gonzaga, Head of Events and Communities at Binance for Brazil and parts
of Latin America, is also a UFSC administration student in her final semester with a
background in IT management. She entered Binance knowing little about
blockchain, having been attracted by the opportunity itself. Her prior experience ¿
internships at creative boutiques and Red Bull, participation in startup
competitions, and leading athletic associations ¿ gave her the event management
and sales skills that secured her role. She now leads community and event
initiatives for the world¿s largest crypto exchange by volume, serving over 300,000
users.
Kais Al-Tabbaa (referred to as ¿Caio¿ by the moderator), Country Manager of
Elibank, brought perhaps the most viscerally personal story. A Syrian national who
fled his country after two years of living through war, he arrived in Brazil as a
refugee with nothing, working simultaneously in restaurants and language services
to save money. When he encountered difficulties sending that money home ¿
losing approximately 30% to banking intermediaries and fees ¿ and subsequently
discovered Bitcoin¿s white paper in 2017, it was not an investment thesis but a
solution to a real, urgent personal problem. That insight drove him to immerse
himself in the ecosystem, moving from trading and community engagement to
business development roles at Neologica (a trading software company) and then
Bitgate, where he grew the Latin American trading volume from $10 million to $2
billion. He has since launched his own company facilitating international fiat-tocrypto transactions and joined Elibank as Country Manager.
Market Opportunity: Scale and Urgency
Francisco framed the market context: the global Web3 market is growing at over
400% annually. Every major Brazilian bank has active stablecoin or tokenization
initiatives. The full chain of infrastructure ¿ blockchains, custodians, exchanges,
analytics firms ¿ is expanding rapidly, as are the Web2 companies that must adapt
or be displaced. For professionals with the right training, this translates to a
genuinely global labor market, with compensation in dollars or euros accessible
from anywhere.
Solange reinforced the scale with concrete figures: the tokenization market
launched in 2020 at roughly 0.5% of global GDP potential, currently sits between
3.5% and 5% depending on the country, and is projected to reach 10% of global
GDP by 2030 ¿ representing approximately $16 trillion. Of that figure, 50% of the
opportunity resides in financial markets (the fastest and most tractable sector),
while real-world assets like real estate account for 20% (larger potential, but more
complex due to the number of stakeholders involved). Former Central Bank
president Campos Neto articulated the ultimate vision: a world where everything is
tokenized and interconnected on a single network, enabling frictionless
transactions between any actors. Solange was unambiguous: ¿Whoever does not
transform their business will become obsolete.¿
A striking data point underscored the revolution already underway in Brazil: 10
million CPF holders (Brazilian tax identification numbers) declare having some type
of digital asset ¿ more than double the 5 million who declare operating on Brazil¿s
stock exchange (B3). This crossover has already happened, quietly.
Real-World Problem-Solving as the Core Discipline
Multiple panelists converged on a single principle: the entry point to value creation
is identifying a genuine real-world pain point, then applying technology as the
solution ¿ not the reverse. Solange illustrated this with a compelling case study
from Paraná: a farmer¿s animal was tokenized as collateral for a bank loan,
dramatically reducing the borrower¿s interest rate. Delivering on this required not
just blockchain for the token, but also a startup with an IoT-enabled smart bracelet
that monitored the animal¿s temperature in real time, continuously verifying that
the collateral remained healthy and had not been sold. The lesson: meaningful
solutions require combining AI, IoT, and blockchain into a coherent system
oriented around user value ¿ lower cost and faster delivery.
Francisco extended this to real estate transactions, describing how atomic smart
contracts can resolve the classic escrow problem ¿ ensuring that money and
property title transfer simultaneously, with neither party at risk of delivering first.
Hamilton introduced a specific cross-border use case: a Paraguayan logistics
company was waiting two business days to convert incoming PIX payments into
dollars. Chainless and its API platform Notos architected a system where incoming
PIX payments are converted to Real stablecoins in real time, and the recipient can
immediately swap into any other desired asset via liquidity pools ¿ ending the wait
entirely. Hamilton articulated a broader vision: just as dollar stablecoins matured to
the point where any asset denominated in dollars could be transacted seamlessly,
the same maturation is occurring for the Real, and ultimately for any tokenizable
asset. His long-term belief is that blockchain is evolving toward a system where any
party can pay in whatever they consider valuable and the recipient can receive in
whatever they consider valuable ¿ a digital evolution of barter, but with instant,
programmable settlement.
Roles, Pathways, and Skills in Demand
The panelists were emphatic that blockchain is not exclusively a developer¿s
domain. The ecosystem needs legal professionals to draft smart contract
frameworks and navigate evolving securities regulation; compliance and custody
specialists; financial analysts designing new tokenization products; community
managers and marketers; business development professionals who can bridge
technical and commercial worlds; and support staff. Kais distilled it: ¿You only need
to translate your talent and identify where it connects with this market.¿
Francisco cited perhaps the most striking supply-demand imbalance in the industry:
there are an estimated only 16,000 blockchain developers globally ¿ against a
demand that is scaling across every industry sector. A senior full-stack developer
with blockchain expertise and English proficiency can command a minimum of
$10,000 per month and receives daily inbound job offers. For students of computer
science and information systems in particular, the opportunity is immediate and
concrete.
Bianca encouraged students not to underestimate the value of non-technical
pathways. She outlined several structured entry points through Binance: the
Binance Academy offers free courses with certificates from global universities. The
Binance Angels Program enables volunteers to support the community, speak at
events, and build public profiles ¿ with top performers invited to the Binance
Blockchain Week in Dubai. The ¿Earn and Learn¿ program rewards users with
crypto for completing Academy lessons. Binance also participates in acceleration
programs analogous to trainee programs, offering fully remote 8-hour-workday
positions on a global team. CZ¿s incubator, Easy Labs, maintains an open application
form, as does the BNB Chain, which accepts project proposals directly.
Hamilton offered a personal note of candor: he left his physics degree when early
crypto earnings exceeded a professor¿s annual salary ¿ and paid dearly for that
decision. His advice was unequivocal: do not drop out. His recommended reading,
Dynamics of Wealth, is designed to help individuals understand their personal
archetype (negotiator, creator, system-builder, etc.) before choosing a professional
path ¿ because self-knowledge is the foundation of effective career positioning.
Francisco highlighted a funding mechanism often unknown to students: blockchain
foundations such as Ethereum, Stellar, and Cardano operate grants programs ¿
funding in tokens, essentially non-dilutive capital ¿ for projects and startups
building on their ecosystems. For anyone with an idea and a development plan,
these grants represent a meaningful alternative to traditional venture funding.
Concluding Remarks and Community Engagement
Francisco Carvalho closed the formal program with a framing that transcended the
event itself. He drew an analogy to a 1998 conference he attended on the TCP/IP
protocol ¿ an event attended by technical specialists, discussing infrastructure that
would ultimately underpin the entire internet, and that no one would ever explicitly
discuss again because it became invisible infrastructure. His prediction: blockchain
will follow the same trajectory. Today, Brazilian national identity is already issued on
blockchain. In a decade, ¿blockchain¿ will not be a conversation topic because it will
simply be the substrate of everything ¿ and the people in that room are
positioned to build the products and companies that will make that transition
happen.
Francisco emphasized that this moment represents ¿the greatest opportunity of this
generation¿ ¿ comparable in scale to the early internet, but now combined with AI,
IoT, RFID, and other converging technologies. Unlike the internet era, however, this
generation has the advantage of seeing it coming clearly enough to prepare
deliberately.
Several practical mechanisms for continued engagement were announced. A QR
code displayed during the event serves as a unified access point: attendees can use
it to obtain their participation certificate, register for event tracking, and receive a
free ticket to the main Blockchain Rio event once ticketing opens. The code also
connects participants to the Blockchain Rio community infrastructure.
Blockchain Rio conducts an annual online Hackathon ¿ now in its fifth year ¿ with
global company partners involved in prizes. The format is explicitly introductory
and multidisciplinary: teams are not required to be composed of developers, and
participants learn from the ground up. Previous editions produced companies that
went on to receive investment. The current university tour ¿ having started in
Curitiba at Fibes and visiting UFSC that day ¿ will continue to the Federal
University of Rio Grande do Sul (Porto Alegre), Unicinos, and UFIS in Vitória, with
the full tour accessible via Blockchain Rio¿s social media channels.
Francisco recommended Fernando Ulrich¿s 2015 book ¿Bitcoin: A Moeda na Era
Digital¿ (available as a free audiobook on YouTube) as foundational reading for
anyone seeking to understand Bitcoin¿s theoretical architecture and, by extension,
blockchain at its core. Solange reinforced the closing message with characteristic
directness: ¿This revolution has no reverse gear. It has started and will only grow.¿
She urged attendees to engage actively ¿ seek out people already in the market,
ask questions, and form opinions through dialogue rather than passivity.
